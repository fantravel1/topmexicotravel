Tequila & Mezcal Guide

Understanding Mexico's Iconic Spirits

Last updated: January 2025

Tequila and mezcal are more than just party drinks—they're cultural treasures with centuries of history, complex production methods, and flavors that rival fine whiskey or wine. Understanding these spirits transforms your Mexico experience, whether you're sipping smoky mezcal in Oaxaca or touring agave fields in Jalisco. This guide covers everything from production to tasting to the best distillery tours.

Tequila vs. Mezcal: What's the Difference?

{<strong>The Simple Answer:</strong> All tequila is mezcal, but not all mezcal is tequila. Mezcal is the broader category—any spirit distilled from agave. Tequila is a specific type of mezcal with strict regulations.</p><p><strong>Tequila:</strong></p><ul><li>Made ONLY from blue Weber agave</p><ul><li>Produced only in designated regions (primarily Jalisco)</p><ul><li>Usually cooked in ovens (industrial or brick)</p><ul><li>Cleaner, often sweeter flavor profile</p><ul><li>Heavily regulated denomination of origin</p><p><strong>Mezcal:</strong></p><ul><li>Can be made from 40+ agave varieties</p><ul><li>Primarily from Oaxaca (but 9 states allowed)</p><ul><li>Traditionally cooked in underground pits (smoky flavor)</p><ul><li>More diverse, artisanal, often smokier</p><ul><li>Less industrial, more small-batch production</p><p><strong>Flavor Profiles:</strong></p><ul><li><strong>Tequila:</strong> Bright, peppery, citrus notes, agave sweetness</p><ul><li><strong>Mezcal:</strong> Smoky, complex, earthy, vegetal, can be fruity or mineral</p><p><strong>The Saying:</strong> "Tequila is to mezcal as bourbon is to whiskey"—a specific, regulated subset of a broader category.}

Understanding Tequila

{<strong>Classifications:</strong></p><p><strong>By Aging:</strong></p><ul><li><strong>Blanco (Silver/Plata):</strong> Unaged or <2 months. Pure agave flavor. Best for cocktails and purists.</p><ul><li><strong>Reposado:</strong> Aged 2-12 months in oak. Mellower, hints of vanilla/caramel. Versatile.</p><ul><li><strong>Añejo:</strong> Aged 1-3 years. Smooth, complex, whiskey-like. Sipping tequila.</p><ul><li><strong>Extra Añejo:</strong> Aged 3+ years. Ultra-smooth, luxury category.</p><ul><li><strong>Joven/Gold:</strong> Usually blanco with coloring/flavoring added. Avoid for quality.</p><p><strong>By Composition:</strong></p><ul><li><strong>100% Agave:</strong> Only blue agave, no added sugars. Always look for this.</p><ul><li><strong>Mixto:</strong> 51% agave, 49% other sugars. Lower quality, causes worse hangovers.</p><p><strong>Production Regions:</strong></p><ul><li><strong>Jalisco:</strong> Primary region, includes Tequila town</p><ul><li><strong>Highlands (Los Altos):</strong> Sweeter, fruitier agave</p><ul><li><strong>Lowlands (Tequila Valley):</strong> Earthier, more herbaceous</p><ul><li>Also: Parts of Guanajuato, Michoacán, Nayarit, Tamaulipas</p><p><strong>Quality Indicators:</strong></p><ul><li>"100% de Agave" on label (required)</p><ul><li>NOM number (distillery registration)</p><ul><li>CRT certification seal</p><ul><li>Avoid anything under $20 (likely mixto or fake)}

Understanding Mezcal

{<strong>Classifications:</strong></p><p><strong>By Production Method:</strong></p><ul><li><strong>Ancestral:</strong> Most traditional. Cooked in pits, crushed by tahona (stone wheel), clay pot distillation. Rare, expensive.</p><ul><li><strong>Artesanal:</strong> Traditional but may use copper stills. Still small-batch, quality-focused.</p><ul><li><strong>Mezcal (Industrial):</strong> Modern equipment allowed. Mass production possible.</p><p><strong>By Aging:</strong></p><ul><li><strong>Joven:</strong> Unaged. Most common. Shows agave character.</p><ul><li><strong>Reposado:</strong> Aged 2-12 months.</p><ul><li><strong>Añejo:</strong> Aged 1+ years.</p><ul><li><strong>Madurado en Vidrio:</strong> Rested in glass (doesn't change flavor, stabilizes).</p><p><strong>Agave Varieties:</strong></p><ul><li><strong>Espadín:</strong> Most common (90% of mezcal). Reliable, approachable.</p><ul><li><strong>Tobalá:</strong> Wild, rare. Complex, floral.</p><ul><li><strong>Madrecuixe:</strong> Wild. Herbaceous, long finish.</p><ul><li><strong>Tepeztate:</strong> Takes 25+ years to mature. Intense, complex.</p><ul><li><strong>Arroqueño:</strong> Large, dramatic. Earthy, chocolatey.</p><ul><li><strong>Cuishe:</strong> Mineral, citrusy.</p><p><strong>Regional Variations:</strong></p><ul><li><strong>Oaxaca:</strong> 85%+ of mezcal. Most diverse.</p><ul><li><strong>Durango:</strong> Growing region, distinct character.</p><ul><li><strong>Guerrero:</strong> Historic, less commercial.</p><ul><li><strong>San Luis Potosí, Puebla, others:</strong> Emerging.}

How to Taste Like a Pro

{<strong>The Right Glass:</strong></p><ul><li><strong>Veladora:</strong> Traditional votive candle holder (yes, really). Opens up aromas.</p><ul><li><strong>Copita:</strong> Small clay cup, traditional for mezcal.</p><ul><li><strong>Wine glass or snifter:</strong> Works well for nosing.</p><ul><li><strong>NOT a shot glass:</strong> You're tasting, not shooting.</p><p><strong>Tasting Steps:</strong></p><p>1. <strong>Look:</strong> Hold up to light. Note color (blanco should be clear, aged will be golden). Swirl—good tequila has "legs" (viscosity).</p><p>2. <strong>Smell (First Nose):</strong> Gentle sniff from distance. Notice initial aromas—agave, fruit, spice, smoke (mezcal).</p><p>3. <strong>Smell (Second Nose):</strong> Bring closer, open mouth slightly. Get deeper aromas. What do you detect?</p><p>4. <strong>Taste:</strong> Small sip. Let it coat your mouth. Notice sweetness, heat, flavors. Swallow and note the finish.</p><p>5. <strong>Add Water (Optional):</strong> A few drops can open up flavors, especially in high-proof mezcal.</p><p><strong>Flavor Vocabulary:</strong></p><ul><li><strong>Tequila:</strong> Pepper, citrus, agave sweetness, vanilla (aged), caramel, herbs</p><ul><li><strong>Mezcal:</strong> Smoke, earth, ash, green pepper, tropical fruit, mineral, leather, chocolate</p><p><strong>Pro Tips:</strong></p><ul><li>Sip, don't shoot (you miss everything)</p><ul><li>No lime and salt needed for quality tequila</p><ul><li>Orange with sal de gusano (worm salt) for mezcal</p><ul><li>Drink slowly—these are sipping spirits}

Distillery Tours: Jalisco

{<strong>Tequila Town (Pueblo Mágico):</strong> The heart of tequila country, 1 hour from Guadalajara.</p><p><strong>Top Tours:</strong></p><p><strong>Casa José Cuervo:</strong></p><ul><li>Oldest distillery (1758)</p><ul><li>Well-organized tourist experience</p><ul><li>Express to full-day options</p><ul><li>Good for beginners</p><p><strong>Casa Herradura:</strong></p><ul><li>Beautiful hacienda setting</p><ul><li>In Amatitán (near Tequila)</p><ul><li>Quality production focus</p><ul><li>Excellent tour experience</p><p><strong>Tequila Fortaleza:</strong></p><ul><li>Small-batch, traditional methods</p><ul><li>Tahona (stone wheel) crushing</p><ul><li>Brick oven cooking</p><ul><li>Serious tequila enthusiasts' choice</p><p><strong>Casa Sauza:</strong></p><ul><li>Historic distillery</p><ul><li>Train tour option</p><ul><li>Good value tours</p><p><strong>José Cuervo Express:</strong></p><ul><li>Scenic train from Guadalajara</p><ul><li>Includes tastings, mariachi</p><ul><li>Full experience in one day</p><ul><li>Book well ahead</p><p><strong>Independent Tours:</strong></p><ul><li>Many agencies offer multi-distillery tours</p><ul><li>Can visit small, artisanal producers</p><ul><li>Private tours allow flexibility</p><p><strong>Tips:</strong></p><ul><li>Weekdays less crowded</p><ul><li>Book tours in advance (especially train)</p><ul><li>Don't drive after—use tour or taxi</p><ul><li>Spring (April-May) sees agave harvesting}

Distillery Tours: Oaxaca

{<strong>Mezcal Country:</strong> Oaxaca is mezcal's heartland. Tours range from large producers to family palenques (small distilleries).</p><p><strong>In Oaxaca City:</strong></p><p><strong>Mezcal Tastings:</strong></p><ul><li><strong>In Situ:</strong> Excellent selection, knowledgeable staff</p><ul><li><strong>Mezcaloteca:</strong> Hundreds of mezcals, educational approach</p><ul><li><strong>La Mezcalerita:</strong> Casual, great variety</p><ul><li><strong>Archive:</strong> Hip bar with quality selection</p><p><strong>Day Trips to Palenques:</strong></p><p><strong>Santiago Matatlán ("World Capital of Mezcal"):</strong></p><ul><li>45 minutes from Oaxaca City</p><ul><li>Dozens of palenques</p><ul><li>Can visit multiple in one day</p><ul><li>See traditional production</p><p><strong>Popular Tours:</strong></p><p><strong>Mezcal Educational Tours:</strong></p><ul><li>Small group, multiple palenques</p><ul><li>Deep dive into production</p><ul><li>Meet producers, learn process</p><ul><li>Half and full-day options</p><p><strong>Alvin Starkman Tours:</strong></p><ul><li>Expert-led, highly regarded</p><ul><li>Access to small family producers</p><ul><li>Customizable experiences</p><p><strong>What You'll See:</strong></p><ul><li>Agave fields (maguey)</p><ul><li>Underground pit roasting</p><ul><li>Tahona crushing (donkey or horse powered)</p><ul><li>Fermentation in wood vats</p><ul><li>Copper or clay pot distillation</p><ul><li>Tasting at source</p><p><strong>Tips:</strong></p><ul><li>Small producers offer most authentic experience</p><ul><li>Buy directly—supports families, best prices</p><ul><li>Ask about rare agaves (tobalá, tepeztate)</p><ul><li>Don't buy from random roadside stands}

Other Mexican Spirits

{<strong>Beyond Tequila & Mezcal:</strong></p><p><strong>Raicilla:</strong></p><ul><li>From Jalisco (mountains near Puerto Vallarta)</p><ul><li>Made from different agaves than tequila</p><ul><li>Funky, complex, less smoky than mezcal</p><ul><li>Growing popularity, worth seeking</p><ul><li>Try in Puerto Vallarta or Guadalajara</p><p><strong>Bacanora:</strong></p><ul><li>From Sonora (northwest Mexico)</p><ul><li>Made from wild agave Pacifica</p><ul><li>Similar to mezcal, regional character</p><ul><li>Protected denomination of origin</p><p><strong>Sotol:</strong></p><ul><li>NOT agave—made from desert spoon plant (Dasylirion)</p><ul><li>From Chihuahua, Coahuila, Durango</p><ul><li>Earthy, herbal, grassy</p><ul><li>Unique category, gaining attention</p><p><strong>Pulque:</strong></p><ul><li>Fermented (not distilled) agave sap</p><ul><li>Ancient drink, pre-Hispanic origins</p><ul><li>Thick, slightly sour, low alcohol (4-8%)</p><ul><li>Try in Mexico City pulquerías</p><ul><li>Acquired taste, cultural experience</p><p><strong>Charanda:</strong></p><ul><li>Sugar cane rum from Michoacán</p><ul><li>Protected denomination</p><ul><li>Similar to rum, local character</p><p><strong>Xtabentún:</strong></p><ul><li>Yucatán anise-honey liqueur</p><ul><li>Sweet, aromatic, after-dinner drink}

Buying Guide

{<strong>What to Look For:</strong></p><p><strong>Tequila:</strong></p><ul><li>"100% de Agave" (always)</p><ul><li>NOM number (distillery ID)</p><ul><li>CRT hologram (authenticity)</p><ul><li>Blanco for mixing, añejo for sipping</p><ul><li>$25-50 for quality bottles</p><ul><li>$50-150 for premium añejos</p><p><strong>Mezcal:</strong></p><ul><li>"100% Maguey" or agave variety named</p><ul><li>Producer name (family or palenque)</p><ul><li>Region of origin</p><ul><li>Production method (artesanal/ancestral preferred)</p><ul><li>Agave variety (espadín most common, wild varieties pricier)</p><ul><li>$30-60 for quality, $80-200+ for rare varieties</p><p><strong>Recommended Brands:</strong></p><p><strong>Tequila:</strong></p><ul><li><strong>Value:</strong> Olmeca Altos, Espolòn, Cimarron</p><ul><li><strong>Mid-Range:</strong> Fortaleza, G4, Pasote, Siete Leguas</p><ul><li><strong>Premium:</strong> Tears of Llorona, Tapatio Excelencia, Casa Dragones</p><p><strong>Mezcal:</strong></p><ul><li><strong>Approachable:</strong> Del Maguey Vida, Banhez, Bozal</p><ul><li><strong>Quality:</strong> Del Maguey Single Village, Vago, Real Minero</p><ul><li><strong>Rare/Premium:</strong> Any single-agave from small producers</p><p><strong>Where to Buy:</strong></p><ul><li>Distilleries (best prices, authenticity)</p><ul><li>Specialty shops in Oaxaca/Tequila</p><ul><li>La Europea chain (major cities)</p><ul><li>Airport duty-free (decent selection, okay prices)</p><ul><li>Avoid random tiendas (counterfeit risk)}

Cocktails to Try

{<strong>Classic Tequila Cocktails:</strong></p><p><strong>Margarita:</strong></p><ul><li>Tequila blanco, lime juice, orange liqueur</p><ul><li>Salt rim optional</p><ul><li>Frozen or on the rocks</p><ul><li>Use quality tequila—it matters!</p><p><strong>Paloma:</strong></p><ul><li>Tequila, grapefruit soda (Squirt/Jarritos), lime, salt</p><ul><li>More popular than margarita IN Mexico</p><ul><li>Refreshing, less sweet</p><p><strong>Tequila Sunrise:</strong></p><ul><li>Tequila, orange juice, grenadine</p><ul><li>Classic for a reason</p><p><strong>Batanga:</strong></p><ul><li>Tequila, Coca-Cola, lime, salt rim</p><ul><li>Simple, surprisingly good</p><ul><li>From La Capilla bar in Tequila town</p><p><strong>Mezcal Cocktails:</strong></p><p><strong>Mezcal Negroni:</strong></p><ul><li>Swap gin for mezcal</p><ul><li>Smoky twist on classic</p><p><strong>Oaxacan Old Fashioned:</strong></p><ul><li>Mezcal, tequila, agave, bitters</p><ul><li>Sophisticated, complex</p><p><strong>Mezcal Margarita:</strong></p><ul><li>Sub mezcal for tequila</p><ul><li>Smokier, more complex</p><p><strong>How Locals Drink:</strong></p><ul><li><strong>Tequila:</strong> Sipped straight (quality), or Paloma</p><ul><li><strong>Mezcal:</strong> Always sipped, with orange slice and sal de gusano</p><ul><li><strong>Shots:</strong> Tourists do it, locals often don't (for quality stuff)}

Mezcal & Food Pairings

{<strong>Pairing Principles:</strong></p><p>Mezcal's complexity pairs wonderfully with food—think of it like wine pairing.</p><p><strong>Espadín (Most Common):</strong></p><ul><li>Grilled meats, especially carne asada</p><ul><li>Tacos al pastor</p><ul><li>Mole negro</p><ul><li>Dark chocolate</p><ul><li>Aged cheeses</p><p><strong>Tobalá (Floral, Complex):</strong></p><ul><li>Seafood ceviche</p><ul><li>Light fish dishes</p><ul><li>Fresh fruits</p><ul><li>Soft cheeses</p><p><strong>Smoky/Intense Mezcals:</strong></p><ul><li>Barbacoa</p><ul><li>Smoked meats</p><ul><li>Chapulines (grasshoppers)</p><ul><li>Strong cheeses</p><ul><li>Dark chocolate with chile</p><p><strong>Traditional Accompaniments:</strong></p><ul><li><strong>Sal de Gusano:</strong> Worm salt with chile, ground agave worms</p><ul><li><strong>Orange Slices:</strong> Citrus complements smoke</p><ul><li><strong>Chapulines:</strong> Crunchy, salty, traditional</p><p><strong>Restaurant Experiences:</strong></p><ul><li>Many Oaxacan restaurants offer pairing menus</p><ul><li>Ask mezcalerías for food recommendations</p><ul><li>High-end Mexican restaurants often have curated selections}

Frequently Asked Questions

What's the worm in the bottle about?

The worm (gusano) is a marketing gimmick from the 1950s, not a sign of quality or tradition. It's actually a moth larva that infests agave plants. Quality mezcal never has a worm. The practice was invented to differentiate brands and appeal to tourists. If you see a worm, it's generally a sign of lower-quality, tourist-targeted mezcal. Skip it and buy something better.

Why is good tequila expensive?

Quality tequila requires blue agave that takes 7-10 years to mature. It's cooked slowly, fermented naturally, and distilled carefully. 100% agave tequila uses no cheap filler sugars. Añejos spend years aging in barrels. The production is labor-intensive and time-consuming. Cheap tequila uses younger plants, added sugars (mixto), and industrial shortcuts. You get what you pay for—quality tequila is worth the investment.

How should I drink mezcal?

Sip it slowly from a wide glass (veladora or copita). Take small sips, letting it coat your mouth. Notice the smoke, fruit, earth, and complexity. Traditional accompaniments are orange slices and sal de gusano (worm salt with chile). Don't shoot it—you miss everything. Add a few drops of water to open up high-proof mezcals. It's a sipping spirit meant to be savored, not slammed.

Where's the best place for a tequila tour?

Tequila, Jalisco—the town the spirit is named for. It's an hour from Guadalajara with dozens of distilleries, from giants like José Cuervo to artisanal producers like Fortaleza. The José Cuervo Express train from Guadalajara offers a full experience with tastings and entertainment. For serious enthusiasts, arrange visits to smaller, traditional producers. Stay overnight to visit multiple distilleries.

Is mezcal stronger than tequila?

Often, but not always. Tequila is typically 35-40% ABV (70-80 proof). Mezcal ranges from 40-55% ABV, with some artisanal and ancestral mezcals reaching higher. The smokiness can also make mezcal seem more intense. However, the strength varies by producer. Always check the label. Higher proof mezcals benefit from a few drops of water to open up the flavors without the burn.
